Mission

The goal of this project is to create a digital library that shows how old wood machines work. I want to save the history of manual skills for the future.Converting antique woodworking machinery into digital 3D assets is a rigorous process of digital preservation. Using **KIRI Engine** and **Polycam**, hundreds of photos are captured to build a raw mesh—a difficult task due to the complex, often reflective surfaces of old iron tools. The workflow concludes in **Blender**, where the "messy" raw data is cleaned and optimized. This involves repairing holes and reducing the file size so the models can run smoothly in a web browser. While the process is time-consuming, it ensures that the physical craftsmanship of the past is accurately archived for a modern, interactive audience.

Technology

Creating this digital archive was a lesson in patience and technical perseverance. My mission was to bridge the gap between antique craftsmanship and the digital age, ensuring that the heritage of manual woodworking isn't lost to time. However, realizing this vision was far more difficult than I anticipated. The most grueling phase was the 3D scanning; I spent two full days battling dark cast iron and complex gears that defied the camera. Using **KIRI Engine** and **Polycam**, I captured hundreds of overlapping photos, frequently restarting due to reflective surfaces or deep shadows. In **Blender**, I faced the "cleanup" struggle, manually repairing noisy meshes and decimating polygon counts so the models would load smoothly in a browser. Finally, coding the interface in **HTML and CSS** required constant troubleshooting to get the scaling just right. Ultimately, this labor-intensive process mirrored the dedication of the craftsmen I’m archiving, proving that digital preservation is itself a manual craft.
